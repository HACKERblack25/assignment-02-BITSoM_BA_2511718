## Database Recommendation

For a healthcare startup’s patient management system, I recommend MySQL (RDBMS). Healthcare data is governed by strict regulatory requirements (like HIPAA) that demand high levels of data integrity and consistency. MySQL follows the ACID (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability) model, ensuring that every transaction—such as updating a patient’s prescription or surgical record—is processed reliably. In the context of the CAP Theorem, MySQL prioritizes Consistency (C) over Availability (A) during a network partition. In healthcare, having perfectly consistent, non-conflicting data is more critical than 100% uptime, as an incorrect medical record could lead to life-threatening errors.
While MongoDB offers flexibility via the BASE model (Basically Available, Soft state, Eventual consistency), its "eventual consistency" is a risk for core patient records where the most recent update must be visible immediately across all systems. MongoDB's schema-less nature is great for rapid prototyping, but the structured, relational nature of patient data—linking patients to doctors, appointments, and billing—is naturally suited for the tabular format of SQL.
However, my recommendation would change if the startup needed to add a fraud detection module. Fraud detection requires analyzing massive volumes of semi-structured data, such as login patterns, IP addresses, and real-time behavioral telemetry, to identify anomalies. For this specific module, MongoDB is superior. It scales horizontally (Partition Tolerance) and can ingest diverse data types at high velocity without the overhead of complex joins.
The ideal modern architecture for this startup would be a polyglot persistence approach: using MySQL as the "Source of Truth" for sensitive patient records and clinical data, while utilizing MongoDB to power the high-speed analytics engine required for the fraud detection module.
